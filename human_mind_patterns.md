# Human Mind Patterns: A Game Design Memory File

This file organizes various patterns of human thought, behavior, and motivation, extracted from psychological models and game design theories. This serves as a "memory file" foundation for building an engaging and humorous game about understanding the human mind.

## 1. Motivational Core (Why Humans Do Things)

Understanding motivation is key to making the game engaging.
- **Intrinsic Motivation:** Doing things for the joy of it. The game should offer autonomy (choice), competence (mastery/skill), and relatedness (social connection or empathy with characters).
- **Extrinsic Motivation:** Doing things for a reward. Achievements, points, unlockables, and funny titles.
- **Operant Conditioning (The "Skinner Box"):** Humans love unpredictable rewards. Variable ratio schedules (like random loot drops or unpredictable funny outcomes to decisions) keep the brain engaged.

## 2. The Bartle Taxonomy of Player Types (Human Archetypes)

Humans approach interactive systems differently. We can categorize the humans (or players in the game) into four main archetypes:
- **Achievers (Diamonds):** Motivated by goals and status. They want to "win" the human mind.
- **Explorers (Spades):** Motivated by curiosity. They want to poke the brain and see what weird thoughts fall out.
- **Socializers (Hearts):** Motivated by relationships. They want to talk to the voices in the head or connect with other players.
- **Killers (Clubs):** Motivated by dominance. They want to manipulate the mind or cause chaotic, funny mental breakdowns.

## 3. Cognitive Biases (The Bugs in the Human Code)

These are mental shortcuts the human brain takes that often lead to irrational behavior. These are perfect for comedic elements in the game:
- **Confirmation Bias:** The brain only sees what it wants to see. (Game mechanic: A character who violently ignores facts that contradict their wild theories).
- **Anchoring Bias:** The brain gets stuck on the first piece of information it receives. (Game mechanic: Convincing a character of something ridiculous by telling them it first).
- **Availability Heuristic:** Believing something is common just because you can easily remember an instance of it. (Game mechanic: A character thinking they will get struck by lightning because they just saw it on TV).
- **Actor-Observer Bias:** "My mistakes are because of bad luck; your mistakes are because you are lazy." (Game mechanic: Hypocritical NPCs).
- **Sunk Cost Fallacy:** Continuing a terrible plan because you already spent time on it. (Game mechanic: A character refusing to stop digging a hole because they've already been digging for hours).
- **Overconfidence Bias:** Thinking you are way smarter or better than you actually are.

## 4. Perception and Engagement

- **Gestalt Principles:** The brain groups things together. We can use this to make the game's UI intuitive or intentionally confusing for comedic effect.
- **Flow State:** The balance between challenge and skill. The game must gradually teach the player how to "hack" the human mind without overwhelming them.

## Game Concept Ideas Based on this Data:
- **"Mind Manager":** A simulation game where you play as the "operator" inside a human's brain, frantically trying to balance their cognitive biases and motivations so they don't do something incredibly stupid in their daily life.
- **"Bias Battler":** A puzzle RPG where you literally fight physical manifestations of cognitive biases using logic, empathy, or just distracting them with shiny objects.
